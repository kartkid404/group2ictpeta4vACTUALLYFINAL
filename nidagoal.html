National Institute on Drug Abuse

NIDA's goal is to further understanding of drug use and addiction and utilize that knowledge to enhance individual and public health.

Goal:

The mission of the National Institutes of Health's National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA) is to "advance science on the causes and consequences of drug use and addiction and to apply that knowledge to improve individual and public health." NIDA serves as a global leader in supporting the majority of research addressing the health-related aspects of drug abuse and addiction. Through its comprehensive programs, NIDA ensures the timely and effective dissemination of cutting-edge research findings to inform policy decisions, enhance clinical practices, and promote public health advancements.

Contributed Research:

Parent Permission and Alcohol Risk Communication in Early Adolescence
Summary:

The study found that parental alcohol risk communication (ARC) reduces adolescent drinking risks, but only for those with prior experience drinking both with and without parental permission. Funded by NIDA, it was published in Addictive Behaviors.

Supersized Alcopop Consumption by College Students
Summary:

This study linked the behavioral economic principle of alcohol demand to Four Loko consumption among college students. Results showed that students favoring large quantities of free alcohol were more likely to consume Four Loko. Pricing and availability restrictions may help protect this at-risk group. Funded by NIDA, the study was published in Experimental and Clinical Psychopharmacology.

Abstinence and Substance Use Among Racial/Ethnic Minority Young Adults During the COVID-19 Global Pandemic
Summary:

The study found that substance use patterns among young adults diverged during the pandemic, with increased abstinence rates and higher-frequency use. Mental health, environment, and impulsivity were key factors, while changes in living situations were protective. Funded by NIAAA and NIDA, it appeared in The American Journal of Drug and Alcohol Abuse.

Young ABCD Study Participants’ Caffeine Use Is Linked to Alcohol Sipping
Summary:

The ABCD Study found that most 9–10-year-olds reported caffeine use (67.6%) and some reported alcohol sipping (22.5%), while other substance use was rare. Early use was linked to demographic factors, externalizing symptoms, and parental AUD history. Funded by NIDA, it appeared in Drug and Alcohol Dependence.

Substance Use and Childhood Adversity Among Young Black Men
Summary:

This study found that alcohol and marijuana use predicted increased depressive symptoms in young Black men (ages 19–22) only when they had experienced adverse childhood events. Funded by NIDA, it was published in The American Journal on Addictions.
